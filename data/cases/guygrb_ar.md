## Geological Setting

The Guy-Greenbrier earthquake swarm occurred in central Arkansas near the town of Guy. The region sits above the Reelfoot Rift, a failed Precambrian rift system with associated fault structures in the crystalline basement. The area had a low to moderate background seismicity rate prior to the swarm onset. The swarm occurred along a northeast-trending fault zone in the basement.

## Industrial Operations

Wastewater disposal in the area was associated with natural gas extraction from the Fayetteville Shale, a productive shale gas play in central Arkansas. Large volumes of produced water co-extracted with shale gas were disposed of via Class II injection wells targeting deep saline formations. Two disposal wells — the Clarita well and the Woolly Hollow well — were identified as the primary candidates for inducing the seismicity based on their proximity and injection histories.

## Earthquake Sequence

The swarm began in late 2010 and included approximately 800–1,000 earthquakes through mid-2011, with the largest event reaching M4.7 on February 28, 2011. The swarm was felt widely across central Arkansas and prompted public concern. Regulators ordered a temporary suspension of injection operations at the nearest wells in response, and the seismicity rate subsequently decreased.

## Attribution in the Published Literature

Horton (2012) concluded that the seismicity was likely induced by the nearby disposal operations. Key evidence:

- Spatial coincidence of the swarm with the two nearest disposal wells
- Temporal correlation with ramp-up of disposal operations
- Seismicity rate decrease following the regulatory suspension of operations
- Hypocentral depths consistent with the disposal interval and underlying basement
- Strike-slip focal mechanisms consistent with regional fault orientation and stress

The operational suspension — a quasi-controlled experiment — provides particularly strong temporal evidence.

## Scientific Controversy

The case is generally accepted as induced seismicity. Attribution to specific wells (Clarita vs. Woolly Hollow) remained a subject of analysis. The Arkansas Oil and Gas Commission ultimately issued rules restricting disposal well operations in the region.
