## Geological Setting

The Landau geothermal project is located in Rhineland-Palatinate, southwestern Germany, within the Upper Rhine Graben — a major continental rift system with an extensional stress regime. The graben has a moderate background seismicity rate associated with the rift structures. The Landau project targets crystalline basement rock at approximately 3 km depth for geothermal heat and electricity generation.

## Industrial Operations

The Landau EGS project operates in a hydrothermal mode, circulating water between two wells (one injector, one producer) to extract geothermal heat from the naturally permeable fractured basement. Operations commenced around 2007. Unlike the Basel project, Landau was not a pure hydraulic stimulation EGS but rather exploited pre-existing natural permeability.

## Earthquake Sequence

A M2.7 earthquake occurred on August 15, 2009, during the operational period of the Landau project. The event was felt by local residents and prompted regulatory review and operational modifications. Subsequent smaller events have also been associated with operations. The Landau case added to European experience with geothermal induced seismicity following the Basel shutdown.

## Attribution in the Published Literature

Published analyses suggested a likely induced origin based on:

- Spatial proximity of the felt event to the geothermal wells
- Above-background seismicity rate during operational periods
- Hypocenter depth consistent with the geothermal reservoir interval
- Normal-faulting focal mechanisms consistent with the Rhine Graben extensional regime

The attribution is complicated by the moderate pre-existing background seismicity in the Rhine Graben.

## Scientific Controversy

The Landau case involves more scientific uncertainty than cases in historically aseismic settings because the Rhine Graben background seismicity requires careful separation of induced from natural events. Some researchers have argued that not all felt events near the facility are necessarily induced.
