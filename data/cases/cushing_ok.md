## Geological Setting

The Cushing earthquake occurred in Payne County, Oklahoma, near the town of Cushing — a major petroleum storage hub that holds a significant fraction of US oil reserves in tank farms. The geological setting is within the Anadarko Basin province, with Paleozoic sedimentary rocks and the Arbuckle Group overlying Precambrian basement. The regional background seismicity had escalated throughout the 2010s in concert with regional wastewater disposal growth.

## Industrial Operations

Multiple wastewater disposal wells operate in the area, injecting produced water from oil and gas operations into the Arbuckle Group. The regional escalation of disposal volumes in the years preceding the earthquake is consistent with the broader Oklahoma induced seismicity pattern documented in the scientific literature.

## Earthquake Sequence

The M5.0 earthquake on November 6, 2016 (coincidentally the same date as the 2011 Prague M5.7 mainshock) caused minor structural damage in Cushing and prompted emergency inspections of the petroleum storage infrastructure. The location near critical national infrastructure elevated the public and regulatory significance of the event.

## Attribution in the Published Literature

Yeck et al. (2017) analyzed the Cushing earthquake in the context of regional induced seismicity. Key evidence:

- Spatial proximity to multiple Arbuckle disposal wells
- Regional disposal-volume-correlated seismicity escalation
- Hypocenter at Arbuckle/basement contact depth
- Strike-slip focal mechanism consistent with the regional stress field
- B-value analysis consistent with fluid-induced seismicity

## Scientific Controversy

The Cushing case is part of the broader Oklahoma induced seismicity pattern. Attribution to specific wells is complicated by the distributed nature of disposal operations across the region.
