## Geological Setting

The Raton Basin is a structural basin straddling the Colorado–New Mexico border, bounded by the Sangre de Cristo Mountains to the west and the Las Animas arch to the east. The basin contains thick sequences of Cretaceous coal-bearing strata (the Vermejo and Raton formations) that host commercially productive coalbed methane. The basement is Precambrian crystalline rock at depths of approximately 2–4 km. The region had a relatively low historical seismicity rate prior to the onset of coalbed methane production and associated wastewater disposal.

## Industrial Operations

Coalbed methane production in the Raton Basin requires dewatering of the coal seams to reduce pore pressure and allow gas to desorb. This process generates large volumes of produced water that must be disposed of. Deep injection disposal wells in the basin inject this water into Paleozoic formations and, in some cases, into basement rock. Injection volumes escalated significantly during the late 1990s and through the 2000s as production activity expanded. Numerous disposal wells were operating in the basin, with some located in the Colorado portion and others in New Mexico.

## Earthquake Sequence

Earthquake activity in the Colorado portion of the Raton Basin began increasing in the late 1990s and continued through the 2000s, culminating in an M5.3 earthquake on August 23, 2011. The sequence included multiple events large enough to be felt by local residents, with felt earthquakes occurring more frequently as disposal volumes grew. The seismicity was concentrated in the northern Raton Basin, in the Colorado portion, spatially coinciding with the highest density of disposal well operations. Prior to the onset of coalbed methane development, the basin had little recorded seismic history.

## Attribution in the Published Literature

Published studies concluded that the Raton Basin seismicity was likely induced by wastewater disposal associated with coalbed methane production. Key evidence cited includes:

- Spatial coincidence of the seismicity cluster with the footprint of disposal well operations
- Temporal correlation between ramp-up in injection volumes and onset of seismicity
- The historically anomalous nature of the seismicity compared to pre-production background
- Hypocentral depths suggesting basement fault reactivation

Gutenberg-Richter b-value analysis of the seismicity cluster was presented in published studies and found to differ from the regional tectonic background, supporting a fluid-induced origin.

## Scientific Controversy

The Raton Basin case is generally accepted as a case of induced seismicity, though attribution of specific events to specific wells is complicated by the distributed nature of disposal operations across the basin. Published studies differ in the degree to which they can discriminate the contributions of individual wells versus the cumulative basin-wide pressure field. The case is also notable because injection wells on both sides of the state border (Colorado and New Mexico) may have contributed to the pressure field.

## Dataset Notes

The RATON case contributes two items in the current 10-item draft, covering evidence tiers 1 and 2. Items for tiers 3 and 4 (adding depth correlation, focal mechanisms, and pressure diffusion modeling) are planned for future dataset expansion. The relatively shallow tier coverage for RATON reflects the state of the published evidence synthesis at the time the current items were drafted — tier 3 and 4 evidence components exist in the literature but were not incorporated into the current draft items.
