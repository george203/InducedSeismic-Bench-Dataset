## Geological Setting

The Preese Hall well targeted the Bowland Shale formation in Lancashire, northwestern England. The shale is at approximately 2.5–3 km depth. The regional tectonic setting is one of moderate historical seismicity associated with Carboniferous fault systems. A fault structure — later mapped as the Preese Hall fault — was present adjacent to the wellbore and was not fully characterized before operations commenced.

## Industrial Operations

Cuadrilla Resources conducted hydraulic fracturing operations at the Preese Hall-1 well in early 2011. The well was the first horizontal shale fracturing well in the UK. Multiple fracturing stages were planned.

## Earthquake Sequence

A M2.3 earthquake occurred on April 1, 2011, during fracturing operations, and was felt locally. A M1.5 event followed on May 27, 2011, after a subsequent fracturing stage. The events prompted the UK Department of Energy and Climate Change to impose a moratorium on hydraulic fracturing, pending investigation. The events were later determined to have been caused by hydraulic communication between the fracturing operations and the adjacent Preese Hall fault.

## Attribution in the Published Literature

The de Pater & Baisch (2011) report and subsequent published studies concluded that fracturing-induced seismicity was the likely cause. Key evidence:

- Stage-by-stage operational correlation between fracturing and seismicity
- Hypocenter locations consistent with the adjacent fault at fracturing depth
- Strike-slip focal mechanisms consistent with the regional stress field
- Coulomb stress analysis showing that injection-induced stress changes were sufficient to bring the fault to failure
- The fault was found to have been intersected or closely approached by the hydraulic fracture network

## Scientific Controversy

The attribution is broadly accepted. The Preese Hall case was influential in establishing regulatory traffic-light protocols for hydraulic fracturing in the UK and internationally. The case demonstrated that even small hydraulic fracturing operations can induce felt seismicity when a critically stressed fault is nearby.
