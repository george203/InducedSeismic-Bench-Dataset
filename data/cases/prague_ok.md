## Geological Setting

The Prague earthquake sequence occurred within the Anadarko Basin, a deep intracratonic sedimentary basin in central Oklahoma. The basement is Precambrian crystalline rock overlain by thick sequences of Paleozoic sedimentary rock. The Wilzetta fault system, a set of northeast-trending strike-slip faults in the Precambrian basement, was identified as the source of the 2011 sequence. Prior to 2009, the region had experienced minimal instrumentally recorded seismicity.

## Industrial Operations

Wastewater disposal in the area is associated with oil production from the Hunton formation and other Paleozoic units. Produced water co-extracted with oil is reinjected into disposal wells targeting the Arbuckle Group, a deep saline formation that directly overlies crystalline basement in central Oklahoma. Injection volumes in the region increased substantially beginning around 2005–2008 as oil production activity expanded. Multiple disposal wells were operating within 5–10 km of the 2011 mainshock hypocenter.

## Earthquake Sequence

The sequence began with a M4.8 foreshock on November 5, 2011, followed by a M5.7 mainshock the next day — the largest earthquake recorded in Oklahoma at that time. An extensive aftershock sequence followed, including a M5.0 event on November 8. The mainshock caused structural damage to buildings in Prague, Oklahoma, and was felt across a broad region. The hypocenters were located at depths consistent with the basement–sedimentary interface, near the base of the Arbuckle disposal interval.

## Attribution in the Published Literature

Published peer-reviewed studies (Keranen et al., 2013; Sumy et al., 2014) concluded that the Prague sequence was likely induced by nearby wastewater disposal. Key evidence cited in these studies includes:

- Spatial coincidence of the mainshock hypocenter with the Wilzetta fault system within ~5 km of active disposal wells
- Temporal correlation between increased disposal volumes and onset of seismicity
- Hypocentral depths consistent with the base of the Arbuckle disposal interval
- Focal mechanism solutions indicating strike-slip faulting on the Wilzetta fault
- Coulomb stress modeling suggesting the foreshock could have triggered the mainshock by stress transfer along the fault system

Pressure diffusion modeling connecting the injection history at specific wells to the timing of seismicity was presented in Keranen et al. (2013), supporting a causal hydraulic link.

## Scientific Controversy

The Prague sequence is generally regarded as a well-documented case of induced seismicity. The primary published debate concerns the relative contributions of different disposal wells and whether the sequence was initiated directly by pore pressure increase or required an intermediate stress transfer step (foreshock triggering mainshock). The broader scientific consensus attributes the sequence to wastewater disposal.

## Dataset Notes

The PRAGUE case contributes four items spanning all four evidence tiers:
- **T1**: Spatial proximity and temporal correlation only — the weakest evidence state
- **T2**: Adds b-value shift from magnitude-frequency analysis
- **T3**: Adds hypocentral depth correlation and focal mechanism solutions
- **T4**: Adds pressure diffusion modeling connecting injection history to seismicity

These four items form a complete tier ladder for this case, making PRAGUE suitable for computing per-case tier sensitivity (Spearman ρ) across the full 1–4 range.
