## Geological Setting

The Pawnee earthquake occurred in north-central Oklahoma within the Anadarko Basin province. The mainshock ruptured the Pawnee fault, a northeast-trending strike-slip structure in the Precambrian crystalline basement underlying the thick Paleozoic sedimentary sequence. The Arbuckle Group — a highly porous and permeable Ordovician carbonate formation that directly overlies crystalline basement — is the primary disposal target for produced water across much of Oklahoma. At the time of the event, the M5.8 mainshock was the largest earthquake recorded in Oklahoma history, subsequently surpassed by the M5.8 Pawnee event tying with the M5.8 Prague earthquake.

## Industrial Operations

Wastewater disposal in the Pawnee area is associated with oil and gas production from multiple formations in the surrounding region. Produced water injected into the Arbuckle Group at multiple disposal wells within approximately 5 km of the mainshock hypocenter. Regional injection volumes had escalated substantially over the years preceding the earthquake as oil production from the SCOOP and STACK plays expanded.

## Earthquake Sequence

The M5.8 mainshock occurred on September 3, 2016, on the Pawnee fault at approximately 5–6 km depth, consistent with the base of the Arbuckle disposal formation and the underlying basement. The earthquake caused structural damage to buildings in Pawnee, Oklahoma, and was felt across a multi-state area. An aftershock sequence followed.

## Attribution in the Published Literature

Published studies concluded that the Pawnee earthquake was likely induced by wastewater disposal. Key evidence includes:

- Spatial proximity of the mainshock hypocenter to multiple Arbuckle disposal wells
- Depth consistent with the Arbuckle/basement contact
- Strike-slip focal mechanism on the Pawnee fault consistent with the regional stress field
- Statistical b-value anomaly in pre-mainshock seismicity
- Pressure diffusion modeling connecting regional injection to the fault location
