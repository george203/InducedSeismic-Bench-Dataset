## Geological Setting

The Azle earthquake swarm occurred in the Tarrant and Parker County area of north Texas, approximately 25 km northwest of Fort Worth. The area is within the Fort Worth Basin, underlain by Paleozoic sedimentary rocks and Precambrian crystalline basement. The Newark East gas field, a major Barnett Shale production area, lies nearby. The region had a low prior seismicity rate, making the swarm anomalous.

## Industrial Operations

The Azle area hosted multiple wastewater disposal wells associated with Barnett Shale gas production, as well as some natural gas production wells. The attribution is complicated by the presence of multiple potential source wells with different injection histories, volumes, and depths. Both disposal into the Ellenburger Group and production-related changes in reservoir pressure have been considered as potential contributors.

## Earthquake Sequence

The Azle swarm began in late 2013 and included approximately 300 felt earthquakes through early 2014, with multiple events exceeding M3 and the largest reaching approximately M3.6. The swarm occurred on a previously unmapped fault system in the area.

## Attribution in the Published Literature

Hornbach et al. (2015) concluded that the seismicity was likely induced by a combination of wastewater disposal and gas production activities. Key evidence:

- Spatial coincidence of seismicity with disposal and production operations
- Seismicity rate broadly correlated with disposal volumes in the area
- Hypocentral depths at disposal formation and basement level
- Strike-slip focal mechanisms consistent with the regional stress field

The multi-well, multi-source geometry makes individual well attribution more difficult than single-well cases such as DFW.

## Scientific Controversy

The attribution of the Azle seismicity to disposal operations is broadly accepted. The multi-source complexity means that the specific roles of individual wells remain less well-constrained than in single-well cases.
