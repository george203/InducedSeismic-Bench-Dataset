## Geological Setting

The Fox Creek area of west-central Alberta lies within the Western Canada Sedimentary Basin, where thick Devonian carbonate and shale sequences overlie Precambrian crystalline basement. The Duvernay Formation — a deep organic-rich shale at approximately 4–5 km depth — is the target for hydraulic fracturing. The formation overlies and is in close proximity to the Rimbey-Meadowbrook reef trend and the underlying basement. The area had a low to moderate background seismicity rate prior to the onset of Duvernay fracturing operations.

## Industrial Operations

Hydraulic fracturing of horizontal wells targeting the Duvernay Formation commenced in the Fox Creek area around 2013. The Duvernay is a tight shale requiring multi-stage hydraulic fracturing for commercial production. Multiple operators conducted fracturing programs in the region.

## Earthquake Sequence

A sequence of felt earthquakes, with multiple events exceeding M3 and the largest reaching M4.4 in January 2016, was associated with hydraulic fracturing operations in the Fox Creek area between approximately 2014 and 2016. The seismicity prompted operational reviews and the development of industry-specific traffic-light protocols in Alberta.

## Attribution in the Published Literature

Schultz et al. (2015, 2017) and related studies concluded that the seismicity was induced by hydraulic fracturing. Key evidence:

- Stage-by-stage correlation between fracturing operations and seismicity onset
- Hypocenters below the Duvernay target, in crystalline basement, consistent with stress transfer or fluid migration to basement faults
- Reverse-faulting focal mechanisms consistent with the regional compressive stress regime in this foreland basin setting
- Spatial coincidence of seismicity with the operational footprint of Duvernay fracturing

The case contributed to the development of the Alberta Energy Regulator's induced seismicity management framework.

## Scientific Controversy

The attribution of the Fox Creek seismicity to hydraulic fracturing is broadly accepted. Scientific discussion has focused on the mechanism by which fracturing at Duvernay depths (~4–5 km) communicates stress or fluid to basement faults at greater depth, and on the role of fault geometry in controlling which operations produce larger events.
