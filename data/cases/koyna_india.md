## Geological Setting

The Koyna Dam is located on the Koyna River in the Deccan Plateau of Maharashtra, India. The plateau consists of thick horizontal basalt lava flows (Deccan Traps) overlying Precambrian crystalline basement. The Deccan Traps are generally considered to be aseismic; the Koyna region had no documented natural seismic history prior to reservoir impoundment. The Koyna seismicity has been associated with reactivation of basement faults beneath the basalt cover.

## Industrial Operations

The Koyna Dam was constructed for hydroelectric power generation and water supply. Reservoir filling began in 1962. The reservoir holds a large volume of water (approximately 2.8 km³ at full capacity) and experiences significant seasonal water level fluctuations due to monsoon precipitation and draw-down for power generation.

## Earthquake Sequence

A M6.3 earthquake struck on December 10, 1967, approximately five years after initial reservoir filling, killing approximately 180 people and injuring thousands more. The earthquake caused severe damage to the nearby town of Koynanagar. The sequence has continued for decades, with additional events exceeding M5 occurring in subsequent years. The Koyna seismic zone has become one of the most extensively studied reservoir-induced seismicity sites in the world.

## Attribution in the Published Literature

The Koyna case is one of the classic reservoir-induced seismicity cases in the global literature. Key evidence for attribution:

- The region had no documented natural seismicity prior to impoundment, despite being part of an extensively monitored seismic network in the broader region
- Temporal onset of seismicity follows initial reservoir filling
- Seasonal water-level-correlated seismicity rate changes over decades of monitoring
- Hypocentral depths consistent with the reservoir base and upper basement
- Focal mechanisms indicating strike-slip faulting consistent with the regional stress field

The M6.3 event is one of the largest earthquakes ever attributed to reservoir impoundment, and the case established the potential for major induced seismicity from large dams in stable continental interiors.

## Scientific Controversy

The attribution is broadly accepted in the scientific community. Ongoing research focuses on:
1. The detailed mechanism (pore pressure diffusion vs. elastic load effects)
2. The three-dimensional geometry of the active fault network
3. Whether the long-running sequence reflects continued induced seismicity or a naturally activated fault that continues to produce aftershocks
